Random information about Sulfuric acid

  • Production of sulfuric acid is and has been linked to the industrial power of a nation.
  • The production of sulfuric acid exists in many ways, such as the wet sulfuric acid process, the lead chamber process, but on this website, the contact process is described.
  • Sulfuric acid is used in a wide variety of ways, such as a domestic acidic drain cleaner, all the way to an electrolyte in lead-acid batteries.
  • Sulfuric acid used to be manufactured at several different concentrations, based on the method used to make it. Nowadays, due to advancements in technology, mainly the 98.3% concentration is made, and all lower concentrations are made by diluting the 98.3% concentration.
  • Sulfuric acid serves as a catalyst for hydrocarbons to "completely combust", in the same manner fire does, and produces "black snakes" which is just the carbon foaming out.

Manufacturing Process

The entire process happens in 4 steps:

  1. Extraction of sulfur
  2. Creation of Sulfur Dioxide
  3. Creation of Sulfur Trioxide
  4. Creation of Sulfur Acid

Main Chemical Properties

  • Highly corrosive. If you value your life, stay clear.
  • Considered a laboratory hazard, even at low concentrations.
  • Non-flammable, but contact creates burns.

Sulfur Extraction

When it comes to preparing the sulfur for the subsequent reactions, one can decide to directly produce sulfur dioxide, or extract sulfur to create sulfur dioxide.

Sulfur can be recovered from natural gas and/or oil, which contain sulfur compounds, some organic, and some are hydrogen sulfide.

Conversion to Sulfur Dioxide

Other than creating sulfur dioxide, occassionally it is acquired through other reactions involving sulfur compounds, rather than diatomic sulfur gas.

Metals often appear in sulfide ore forms, and during their manufacturing process, sulfur dioxide is created as a leftover product. One such sulfide ore are pyrites, which are iron-based.

4Fe$_{2(s)} + $ 11O$_{2(g)} \,\to\,$ 2Fe$_2$O$_{3(s)}$
S$_{(s)}$ + O$_{2(g)} \,\to\,$ SO$_{2(g)}$

Both sulfide ores and solid sulfur follow the same process.They react with oxygen gas in furnaces at around 1300 K to make sulfur dioxide.

The resulting gas is about 10% sulfur dioxide and 10% oxygen by volume, and has to be passed through heat exchangers to cool it down to 700 K, creating steam in the process.

This way, for each ton of sulfuric acid, about one ton of high pressure steam is also produced.

Conversion to Sulfur Trioxide

This exothermic, reversible reaction is the key to the entire process.

2SO$_{2(g)}$ + O$_{2(g)} \rightleftarrows $ 2SO$_{3(g)}$
$\Delta$H$^\circ$ = -192 $\frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{mol}}$

The process is as follows:

  1. Sulfur dioxide and oxygen from air are taken in a 1:1 ratio (by volume)
  2. 400$^\circ$ C - 450$^\circ$ C, 1 - 2 atm, and also a Vanadium (V) Oxide catalyst is used.
  3. Sulfur trioxide (hopefully) is produced

Considerations

As much sulfur dioxide as possible needs to be converted, as any potential release of sulfur dioxide can cause acid rain. Naturally, creating more sulfur trioxide is more financial beneficial for the company, so they want to optimize the reaction as best as possible.

In the reaction, the choice of 1:1 reactants by volume is very specific. Despite the fact that there is a 2:1 mole ratio between the sulfur dioxide and oxygen gas, we use Le Chatelier's principle to use the excess of oxygen to shift the equilibrium to the right.

So why not have an excessively large amount of oxygen? Ignoring the feasibility of acquiring said oxygen, an enormous amount of oxygen (say 1:1000 ; SO$_2$:O$_2$) would definitely cause very few effective collisions to occur. Since there are mostly oxygen molecules, which can't create sulfur trioxide by themselves, it would be futile to increase the oxygen further.

Through rigorous empirical testing, the 1:1 volume ratio of reactants proved to be the best possible ratio for best overall yield of sulfur trioxide.

The temperature is similarly carefully chosen. As the reaction is exothermic, lowering the temperature would shift the equilibrium to the right. Evidently, 400$^\circ$ C is not low. If you recall, the higher the temperature, the higher the rate of reaction. This means that while having a low temperature would benefit the concentration of the resultant sulfur trioxide, it would take an excessively long time to create said sulfur trioxide.

Thus, 400$^\circ$ C is considered a compromise between two extremes, chosen empirically as well.

Lastly, the pressure. Notice the moles on each side of the equilibrium equation. According to Le Chatelier's, you would want to increase the pressure. Turns out, atmospheric pressure is still enough to get a high efficiency in the reaction.

The overall reaction still has approximately a 99.5% conversion. If we were to increase the pressure, it would be better, but that just isn't financially efficient.

Conversion to Sulfuric Acid

Contrary to what one might think, the last step does not involve just the addition of water. If water was reacted with sulfur trioxide, it creates a fog of sulfuric acid, both harmful, and hard to control. The reason being is that this reaction is a highly exothermic reaction, meaning it would create the product in gaseous form.

H$_2$SO$_{4(aq)}$ + SO$_{3(g)} \,\to\,$ H$_2$S$_2$O$_{7(aq)}$
H$_2$S$_2$O$_{7(aq)}$ + H$_2$O $\,\to\,$ 2H$_2$SO$_{4(aq)}$

The first reaction is to dissolve the sulfur trioxide in sulfuric acid, creating fuming sulfuric acid or oleum, which can then be safely reacted with water.
